## 2. The "Working Backwards from the Invoice" Methodology

### Core Principle
Instead of beginning with product design and hoping sales will follow, the Working Backwards methodology starts at the moment of the first invoice — then works back through every commercial step required to reach that moment. The invoice is not symbolic; it signals that the customer has agreed to terms, systems are ready, and the product is delivering value.

**The key insight:** What sells isn't always what's technically best. It's what fits buyers' constraints, workflows, and purchasing logic. Understanding those constraints must shape the product roadmap, not just the sales strategy.

### The Six Building Blocks (in reverse order, from invoice back to design)

```
1. Contracting and Billing     ← closest to the invoice
2. Sales Enablement
3. Procurement
4. Product Infrastructure
5. Commercial Fit
6. Product Design              ← furthest from the invoice
```

Each building block defines requirements for the one before it. Buyers evaluate products through all six lenses simultaneously — a gap in any layer blocks the deal.

## 3. Building Block 1: Contracting and Billing

### The Commercial Mindset
Design your entire process, infrastructure, and offer with the invoice as the endpoint. Before you send an invoice, the customer must have agreed to your terms, your systems must be operational, and your product must be delivering value.

The full sales process includes: targeting accounts → first contact → management pitch → clinical/admin/risk evaluations → IT review → pre-sales assessments → formal offer and terms sheet → contract negotiations → acceptance and implementation.

### US Contracting: Required Documents

When selling clinical software in the US, contracting is a multi-document, multi-team process that typically takes months:

1. **Customer Proposal / Letter of Intent** — Outlines scope, pricing, and terms before formal agreement
2. **Master Services Agreement (MSA)** — Governs the overall relationship; defines ownership for maintenance, compliance, and dispute resolution
3. **Order Form / Pricing Schedule** — Product-specific pricing, volume tiers, and delivery milestones
4. **Business Associate Agreement (BAA)** — HIPAA-required if handling any Protected Health Information (PHI); defines data handling obligations
5. **Service Level Agreement (SLA)** — Defines response times, uptime guarantees, update cycles, support processes, and escalation paths
6. **Data Security Acknowledgment / Security Policy Acknowledgment** — Buyer's confirmation that your security posture meets their standards
7. **Implementation Plan** — Detailed timeline and responsibilities for deployment, onboarding, training
8. **Subcontractor Addendum** — Discloses and governs any third-party subcontractors involved in delivery
9. **Insurance Certificates** — Proof of adequate liability and professional indemnity insurance

### German / EU Contracting: Required Documents

For connected medical devices operating in Germany:
1. **Master Services Agreement (MSA)** — Commercial terms
2. **Data Processing Agreement (AVV / Auftragsverarbeitungsvertrag)** — Required under GDPR and BDSG for any data processing
3. **CE Marking documentation and clinical evaluation summary** — Prerequisite for any regulated medical device
4. **Cybersecurity and technical compliance documentation** — Mandatory for devices processing health data in cloud environments
5. **National and regional public procurement law compliance** — Must follow formal tender requirements in many cases

### US Reimbursement Pathway
**Who pays:** CMS/Medicare/Medicaid, commercial insurers, hospitals directly (capital budgets), or patients (direct pay).

**Step-by-step to get paid:**
1. Identify target payer (CMS vs. commercial vs. hospital vs. patient)
2. Obtain correct CPT (Current Procedural Terminology) or HCPCS (Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System) codes
3. Register for NPI (National Provider Identifier)
4. Compile economic evidence demonstrating clinical value and cost-effectiveness
5. Apply for coverage determination from the relevant payer
6. Secure coverage approval
7. Submit coded invoice (including NPI, service location, device class for CMS billing; revenue codes and applicable modifiers)
8. Match invoice to PO, delivery records, and usage logs
9. Respond to payer information requests

**Key US reimbursement structure:**
- **Medicare/Medicaid (CMS):** Requires formal coverage determination; economic evidence is mandatory
- **Commercial Insurance:** Individual payer negotiations; coverage varies by plan and network
- **Hospital Direct Purchase (capital budgets):** Requires Value Analysis Committee (VAC) approval; no external payer involved
- **Group Purchasing Organizations (GPOs):** Negotiate contracts on behalf of hospital networks; getting onto a GPO contract accelerates hospital sales

**Coding systems:**
- **CPT codes** — Physician and outpatient services
- **HCPCS codes** — Devices, supplies, and services not in CPT
- **DRG (Diagnosis-Related Group)** — Hospital inpatient payment system; your device's clinical impact must map to DRG cost and outcome metrics
- **APC (Ambulatory Payment Classification)** — Hospital outpatient payment system

### German Reimbursement Pathway
**Inpatient:** G-DRG system. New technologies must apply for a **NUB (Neue Untersuchungs- und Behandlungsmethoden)** designation to get add-on payments above the base DRG. The application window is annual (typically October–November for the following year).

**Outpatient (statutory insurers / GKV):** Must be listed in the statutory insurance catalogue. Billing uses:
- **OPS codes** (Operationen- und Prozedurenschlüssel) — Procedure coding
- **EBM codes** (Einheitlicher Bewertungsmaßstab) — Outpatient physician billing
- **ICD codes** — Diagnosis coding

**DiGA (Digitale Gesundheitsanwendungen):** Digital health applications pathway under the HHVG/DVG legislation. Getting listed in the DiGA directory at BfArM (Federal Institute for Drugs and Medical Devices) enables statutory reimbursement, but the prescribing and payment pathway has created operational complexity in practice. Listing does not guarantee revenue — the prescribing workflows between physicians, patients, and insurers must also be operationalized.

**Statutory Insurance Funds (GKV) billing routes:**
- Hospitals: bill via DRG codes
- Home-use devices: billed through certified DME (durable medical equipment) suppliers
- Direct billing: only if the company has a direct contract with the insurer

**Key German billing codes and systems:** OPS (procedures), EBM (outpatient billing), ICD (diagnoses), G-DRG (inpatient), NUB (new technology add-on), EBM modifier codes for special services.

### Value Analysis Committees (VAC)
VACs are internal hospital committees — typically including clinical, financial, procurement, IT, and risk stakeholders — that evaluate new products before approving purchase. They do not evaluate innovation; they evaluate value, risk, and fit within existing systems. To succeed at VAC:
- Present a clear economic case (cost savings, revenue impact, efficiency gains)
- Demonstrate clinical evidence (peer-reviewed data, real-world outcomes)
- Address IT and data security requirements upfront
- Provide a total cost of ownership model, not just purchase price
- Have an internal clinical champion who can advocate within the committee

## 4. Building Block 2: Sales Enablement

### What Sales Enablement Is
Sales enablement is the service design around the sales team — the systems, knowledge, and structure that make selling possible. It defines what the team can do independently, where they need support, and how the organization moves together to close deals. Without it, even excellent products stall in the pipeline.

### Key Elements of a Sales-Ready Operation
- **Pricing structure:** Clear pricing frameworks, pre-approved terms, standard documentation. Sales teams must be able to quote with confidence without escalating every decision.
- **Onboarding and training materials:** Customer-facing guides, product tutorials, and clinical orientation programs.
- **Implementation guides:** Step-by-step deployment documentation for IT and clinical teams at buyer organizations.
- **Compliance documents:** Pre-packaged security documentation, certifications, and regulatory compliance summaries that can be rapidly produced during procurement.
- **Procurement-ready documentation:** RFP/tender response templates, legal boilerplate, and technical appendices that can be adapted quickly.

### Internal Handoffs
Every stage of the deal must have a defined internal owner. Map from first contact through to post-sale delivery:
- Assign response expectations at each stage
- Document what information travels with each handoff (so nothing is lost between sales, product, and operations)
- Maintain shared visibility through a single CRM or tracking system
- Buyers experience continuity; teams avoid handoff chaos

### The Sales Playbook
A strong playbook captures proven approaches and makes them teachable and repeatable:
- Define stages: outreach → demo → proposal → close → post-sale
- Capture proven messaging and objection handling
- Standardize demo formats and success criteria
- Update based on real outcomes and field feedback

**US sales team to start:** One experienced field rep with provider or hospital sales experience; a part-time customer success lead; inside sales or founder-led outreach for early targeting; basic materials (pricing sheet, value slide, FAQ).

**German sales team to start:** German-speaking sales manager familiar with hospital procurement; local support for documentation and onboarding; clinical advocates or Key Opinion Leaders (KOLs) for credibility; emphasis on professionalism, clarity, and patience.

### Organizational Readiness
Sales enablement doesn't end at contract signature:
- Align delivery, onboarding, and training plans before closing the deal
- Define how customer success supports the transition from sales to implementation
- Confirm IT, data, and compliance readiness before launch
- Track post-sale performance and use feedback to refine the process
- Buyers notice responsiveness, coordination, and ease of getting started — these experiences determine renewals and expansion

## 5. Building Block 3: Procurement

### How Procurement Really Works
Procurement exists to protect the organization, not to evaluate clinical or technical merit. Procurement teams translate internal needs into purchasing actions and filter out anything that doesn't meet internal standards. They rarely choose who wins — but they frequently decide who never gets through the door. They are trained to look for completeness, clarity, and compliance.

**Critical insight:** Legal, finance, and IT must all say "yes" before procurement approves a purchase. Clinician enthusiasm is necessary but not sufficient. If procurement can't buy it, the product is not market-ready, regardless of its clinical value.

### The Procurement Stack
For any healthcare organization, the procurement process involves:
- **Clinical evaluation** — Does it work? Does it fit clinical workflows?
- **IT/Security review** — Data security, cloud hosting, integration architecture, access controls, incident response
- **Legal review** — Liability, privacy law compliance (HIPAA, GDPR), IP ownership, indemnification
- **Finance/Budget alignment** — Does it fit available budget categories? Capital vs. operating expense?
- **Risk assessment** — What happens if the vendor fails? What's the exit plan?

### Designing for the Procurement Process
Mirror the structure of RFPs and tender documents in your sales materials:
- Use standardized responses for compliance and certification sections (pre-approved boilerplate saves weeks)
- Prepare financial and technical appendices in advance
- Maintain a library of reusable RFP components ready for adaptation
- Create a cross-functional internal RFP/tender response team (legal, IT security, product management)
- Track deadlines, submission formats, and evaluation criteria
- Review every response post-submission to improve the next one

### Hospital Procurement Dynamics (US)
- Individual hospitals make purchasing decisions for most items
- GPOs (Group Purchasing Organizations) negotiate contracts on behalf of hospital networks — getting on a GPO contract creates access but doesn't guarantee sales
- Capital budgets (large one-time purchases) require different approvals than operating budgets (recurring costs)
- VAC approval is typically required for anything above a threshold spend

## 6. Building Block 4: Product Infrastructure

### What Infrastructure Really Means in Healthcare
Infrastructure is the machinery that transforms a product into something buyers can depend on. It includes every process connecting your product to its users: logistics, onboarding, IT compatibility, service response, regulatory upkeep. Hospitals evaluate not just what the product does, but whether the vendor can keep it running, keep it safe, and keep it compliant.

Infrastructure components:
- **Delivery and logistics** — How the product reaches the customer; installation, setup, and configuration workflows
- **Tier 1 support / replacements / recall procedures** — Defined escalation paths for issues
- **Training modules and certification programs** — Clinical and operational onboarding for end users
- **Customer onboarding workflows and reference guides** — Step-by-step documentation for implementation teams
- **Integration tools and data templates** — Technical kits for hospital IT departments
- **Field service or remote support** — Ongoing technical support coverage

### EMR / EHR Integration (the "EMR Moat")
More than 75% of US hospitals use three major EMR systems (Epic, Cerner/Oracle Health, Meditech). The strategic imperative is to **complement, not replace** these systems. Integration strategies:

1. **Web Frames / iFrames** — Embed your application within the EMR interface using web frames. Lowest integration effort; keeps your product visible within clinical workflow without replacing the EMR. Limitations: some security policies restrict iFrames.

2. **Headless Applications** — Your application runs in the background, communicating with the EMR via API, but the UI is served separately. Works well for workflow augmentation tools.

3. **API-Driven Integration via FHIR** — The preferred modern standard. FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) is the HL7-endorsed API standard for health data exchange. Key FHIR capabilities:
   - Read/write patient data from the EMR
   - Trigger workflows based on clinical events
   - Submit clinical documentation back into the patient record
   - US regulations (21st Century Cures Act) mandate FHIR API access for certified health IT
   - SMART on FHIR enables secure OAuth-based app authorization within EMR environments

**Practical advice:** Start with the EMR sandbox environment early. Budget 6–18 months for EMR integration, depending on the system and use case. Epic's App Orchard, Cerner's Code program, and similar vendor ecosystems provide structured paths for third-party app certification.

### Making Infrastructure Commitments Real (Contract Alignment)
Your MSA and SLA should describe what delivery looks like in practice:
- Reflect onboarding timelines and training formats in the SLA
- Specify response times, update cycles, and support processes
- Use the MSA to define ownership for maintenance and compliance
- Document shared responsibilities between vendor and buyer
- Ensure onboarding is consistent whether delivered in-house or by outsourced partners

## 7. Building Block 5: Commercial Fit

### What Commercial Fit Means
Commercial fit is where a product's value meets the economic reality of its buyers. It's the point where clinical outcomes connect to budgets, workflows, and financial constraints. The focus shifts from technical validation to proving that the product can sustain itself in the market: priced correctly, supported by evidence, aligned with how buyers make purchasing decisions.

### Health Economics and HEOR
Health Economics and Outcomes Research (HEOR) translates clinical outcomes into financial terms that buyers understand. DHW partners with Avalon Health Economics for this work. Economic validation is not optional — it is a core GTM requirement, not an afterthought.

**Key HEOR deliverables:**
- **Budget impact analysis** — What does adopting this product cost vs. save the health system over 1–3 years?
- **Cost-effectiveness analysis** — Cost per quality-adjusted life year (QALY), cost per avoided adverse event, etc.
- **Return on investment (ROI) model** — Revenue generated, costs avoided, FTE savings, or length-of-stay reduction
- **Real-world evidence** — Outcomes data from actual patient populations, not just controlled trials

**Framing value for buyers:**
- Hospitals care about: length of stay reduction, readmission rates, FTE (staff time) savings, revenue cycle impact, bed utilization
- Payers care about: total cost of care, avoidance of high-cost events, formulary impact
- Physicians care about: clinical outcomes, workflow fit, time savings
- Finance departments care about: ROI, total cost of ownership, budget category (capex vs. opex), multi-year contract value

**Critical insight on impact measurement:** The real beneficiaries of a technology may not be the patients but the caregivers or providers. Example: Elder care technology that reduces caregiver cognitive workload reduces burnout and turnover — which directly improves provider financial performance. The economic case must follow the money to whoever bears the cost or captures the benefit.

### Pricing Strategy
Pricing must align with how the market expects to buy and how the company plans to scale.

**Pricing models:**
- **Unit-based / per-device** — One-time purchase; common for capital equipment
- **Subscription / SaaS** — Recurring annual or monthly fee; common for software and digital health
- **Usage-based / per-transaction** — Pay per test, per patient, per procedure; aligns cost with utilization
- **Bundled / hybrid** — Device plus service contract; common for complex systems
- **DTC (direct-to-consumer)** — Bypasses institutional procurement; relevant where patients pay directly

**Pricing considerations:**
- Align with buyer budget cycles (hospitals typically finalize capital budgets mid-year for the following fiscal year)
- Account for reimbursement differences across markets (reimbursed vs. out-of-pocket)
- Test affordability and budget fit before finalizing price
- Decide early on revenue model and stick to it — late pivots confuse procurement and payers

### Validating Commercial Fit
Commercial fit validation is structured evidence gathering, not just market research:
- Define specific questions before starting (What drives approval? What triggers rejection?)
- Engage real buyers to test purchasing logic and budget alignment
- Run pilot projects with post-pilot conversion paths pre-mapped (not open-ended trials)
- Track objections, approval timelines, and procurement hurdles as data
- Use findings to refine pricing and product strategy before scaling

## 8. Building Block 6: Product Design

### Design Starts Where the Transaction Ends
Working Backwards requires that commercial requirements actively shape the product roadmap. The most technically advanced product fails if it can't be bought, billed, and operationalized within the buyer's existing systems.

**Practical implications for product teams:**
- Map the product's existence within the hospital system before finalizing feature scope
- Consider the full cast of stakeholders in the sales process, not just end users
- Ensure the product can generate the evidence needed for VAC approval and payer coverage decisions
- Build in data export capabilities that support billing codes and clinical documentation
- Design onboarding complexity to fit what buyers will actually fund and staff
- Consider procurement requirements (security certifications, FHIR compliance, MDR classification) as product requirements, not afterthoughts

## 9. The Nine "Working Backwards" Moves

A practical framework from DHW's core methodology article. These nine moves take a company from product concept to first revenues:

1. **Invoice Readiness** — Can you actually send an invoice? Are your billing codes, NPI, payment systems, and legal entities in place? This is where most companies discover they aren't as ready as they thought.

2. **Hospital Procurement Readiness** — Do you have the documentation stack that procurement needs? Security certs, vendor registration, insurance, compliance docs, and a vendor portal profile.

3. **Value Analysis Committee Preparation** — Have you built the clinical and economic evidence package a VAC needs to approve purchase? Peer-reviewed evidence, economic model, implementation plan, clinical champion strategy.

4. **Implementation Planning** — Is there a credible, documented plan for getting the product live at a customer site? Timelines, responsibilities, training, go-live support, and escalation paths.

5. **FHIR Integration** — Is the product integrated with the EMR ecosystem in a way that doesn't require significant IT project investment from the buyer? FHIR API integration, SMART on FHIR authorization, and sandbox testing.

6. **Commercial Fit Validation** — Has the value proposition been tested with real buyers? Has the pricing held up under actual budget scrutiny?

7. **Artifact-to-Requirement Translation** — Are sales artifacts (pitch decks, proposals, demos) translated into product requirements? Does the product actually deliver what is being sold?

8. **Sales Enablement** — Are the playbooks, pricing guides, objection handlers, and onboarding materials built and in the hands of the sales team?

9. **Gates and Metrics** — Are there clear go/no-go criteria at each stage of the commercial process? Are the right metrics being tracked (sales cycle length, VAC approval rate, pilot-to-contract conversion, etc.)?

## 10. Moving Beyond a Pilot: Why Pilots Fail to Scale

### The "Pilot Purgatory" Problem
Many digital health companies accumulate pilot deployments without converting them to paid contracts. Pilots drain resources and confuse investors when there's no conversion path. A pilot that succeeds clinically but fails commercially is not a success.

### Why Pilots Fail to Scale
1. **No path to payment** — The payment infrastructure (billing codes, payer coverage, hospital budget line) was never established before the pilot started.
2. **User success vs. buyer requirements mismatch** — The pilot measured what users liked, not what buyers need to justify a purchase (economic outcomes, budget impact, procurement compliance).
3. **Evidence gaps** — The pilot collected the wrong data, or no data, to satisfy VAC or payer requirements.
4. **No post-pilot plan** — The pilot ended without a defined next step: who needs to approve the contract, at what price, under what terms.

### How to Structure Pilots for Conversion
- **Define success metrics for the buyer, not just the user** — Before starting, agree with the buyer on what data will trigger a purchase decision.
- **Pre-negotiate the post-pilot path** — Know the contract terms, budget source, and approval process before the pilot starts.
- **Choose alpha pilot sites strategically** — Select sites that can become commercial references and have active internal champions.
- **Build value for both users and buyers simultaneously** — Users need the product to work in their workflow; buyers need economic evidence.
- **Identify and cultivate internal champions** — A clinical champion alone is not enough; you need a financial or administrative champion who controls the budget.
- **Measure what matters to buyers** — Cost reduction, time savings, revenue impact, risk reduction, readmission rates.

## 11. Clinical and Commercial Validation

### The Validation Gap
More than 40% of digital health products lack adequate clinical trials. Lack of validation is a leading cause of regulatory rejection, payer coverage denial, and VAC rejection.

### Three-Stage Validation Framework
1. **Verification (Engineering Validation)** — Does the product do what it's designed to do in controlled laboratory conditions? Benchmarking, unit testing, performance testing. Does not demonstrate clinical efficacy.

2. **Analytical Validation (Controlled Conditions)** — Does the product perform accurately and reliably in a clinical-like setting? For diagnostic devices: sensitivity, specificity, positive/negative predictive value. For software: algorithmic performance on defined datasets.

3. **Clinical Validation (Efficacy in Context of Use)** — Does the product improve outcomes when used by real patients or clinicians in real healthcare settings? This is the standard required by payers, VACs, and most regulatory submissions.

### Validation vs. Impact Measurement
**Validation** asks: does the product work?  
**Impact measurement** asks: does the product change outcomes for the relevant stakeholders?

These are distinct activities with distinct methodologies. A product can be validated (it does what it says) without demonstrating impact (it doesn't move the needle on outcomes that matter to buyers). The impact measurement must identify the correct beneficiary — who actually gains when the product works as intended?

**Example:** An elder care technology reduces the cognitive workload on caregivers. The validated outcome might be reduced care errors. The real economic impact is on caregiver burnout and turnover — which affects provider staffing costs and financial performance. Measuring and communicating this impact (in dollars saved on turnover and recruitment) creates a compelling business case that the technology itself does not automatically generate.

### Regulatory Pathways (Overview)
**US:**
- **FDA 510(k) clearance** — Predicate-based pathway for most class II medical devices; demonstrates substantial equivalence to a predicate device
- **FDA De Novo** — For novel low-to-moderate risk devices without a predicate
- **FDA PMA (Premarket Approval)** — High-risk class III devices; requires robust clinical data
- **SaMD (Software as a Medical Device)** — FDA has issued specific guidance; risk classification depends on intended use and potential patient harm
- **DTx (Digital Therapeutics)** — Subset of SaMD; may require clinical trial evidence equivalent to a pharmaceutical trial

**EU/Germany:**
- **CE Marking under MDR (Medical Device Regulation 2017/745)** — Required for all medical devices in the EU; substantially more rigorous than the predecessor MDD
- **IVDR (In Vitro Diagnostic Regulation 2017/746)** — For diagnostic devices
- **DiGA Pathway** — German-specific digital health application reimbursement pathway under DVG/DIGA legislation; managed by BfArM. Provides a "fast-track" provisional reimbursement option while evidence is being generated.

## 12. Go-to-Market Strategy for Medical Devices

### Value Proposition Development
A compelling healthcare value proposition must answer:
1. What clinical problem does this solve? (And for whom specifically?)
2. What is the evidence that it solves it? (Validation stage)
3. What does solving it save or generate economically? (HEOR stage)
4. Why is this better than existing alternatives or the status quo?
5. Why is the timing right now?

### Sales Channels in Healthcare
- **Direct sales** — Company-employed field reps selling directly to hospitals, health systems, or physician groups. High control, high cost. Appropriate for complex, high-value products.
- **Distribution partnerships** — Working with established medical device distributors who have existing hospital relationships and procurement relationships. Faster market access, lower control.
- **GPO contracts** — Enables selling to a large pool of hospitals through a single contract. Required by many health systems for vendor approval.
- **Digital/remote sales** — Appropriate for lower-complexity software products; reduces CAC (customer acquisition cost) but may require in-person support for VAC and IT reviews.
- **KOL (Key Opinion Leader) strategy** — Engage clinical thought leaders to generate peer influence, publication opportunities, and speaking platforms.

### Physician Engagement
- Clinical champions within hospitals are essential but not sufficient — they must be connected to budget holders.
- Continuing Medical Education (CME) programs can create structured physician engagement at scale.
- Medical conferences and professional society relationships build credibility.
- Physician-to-physician communication (referrals, publications, presentations) is more persuasive than vendor marketing.

### US Healthcare Market Dynamics
- Relationship-driven market; trust and clinical credibility are prerequisites
- Hospital consolidation means fewer but larger buying entities (IDNs — Integrated Delivery Networks)
- IDN-level contracts can create system-wide access but require more complex negotiations
- GPO alignment affects hospital purchasing decisions for most categories
- Medicare/Medicaid coverage decisions create reference points for commercial payer decisions

### European/German Market Dynamics
- More centralized procurement in many cases (statutory insurance, federal regulations)
- Germany's GKV (gesetzliche Krankenversicherung) covers ~90% of the population; private insurers (PKV) cover the rest
- Hospital procurement follows public tender rules for above-threshold contracts
- DiGA pathway provides a structured (if bureaucratic) route for digital health applications
- Local language, local relationships, and local regulatory knowledge are prerequisites
- NUB applications are the primary route for novel inpatient technologies to receive add-on payments

## 13. Common Pain Points in Medtech Commercialization

These are the exact frustrations that DHW's clients and prospective clients bring to the table:

### Pain Point 1: "Great product, but the sales team can't sell it"
Clinician-founders assume that clinical excellence drives adoption. It doesn't. Unqualified sales reps, poor sales strategy, or hiring a large sales team before understanding the buyer creates wasted effort and misalignment between product and sales. The product's commercial system must be designed before the sales team is built.

### Pain Point 2: Procurement and IT blocking the sale
Clinician enthusiasm is not sufficient. Even loved products fail at hospital procurement because of data compliance issues, MDR/510(k) classification gaps, lack of EMR integration, or missing security certifications. Without the right infrastructure (vendor approvals, security certs, data processing agreements), sales teams get stuck in procurement limbo despite clinical interest.

### Pain Point 3: The pilot project that never converts to revenue ("Pilot Purgatory")
Dozens of pilots, zero real sales. Pilots are relatively easy to secure, but without a conversion path — predefined success metrics, budget source, procurement plan — they drain resources and confuse investors. A medtech consultant observation: "If you don't pre-define success metrics, budget, and procurement plan, you risk languishing in endless pilots with no deal at the end."

### Pain Point 4: Disconnect between builders and sellers
Engineers and clinicians design brilliant products that fail on the clinic floor because they don't reflect actual frontline workflows. Product teams build in silos; sales teams discover the gaps during demos. This misalignment produces finger-pointing between product and commercial teams when adoption disappoints.

### Pain Point 5: Clinically excellent but commercially inviable
"Clinically we had great results, but we couldn't get paid." The canonical example is Pear Therapeutics — the first FDA-approved digital therapeutic, prescribed by providers, but not reimbursed by insurers due to lack of billing codes. Pear went bankrupt in 2023. A product can be medically brilliant and commercially unsustainable if the reimbursement model isn't established in parallel with the product.

### Pain Point 6: Local market hurdles (regulatory/commercial fit)
EU and UK markets present structural barriers. Getting listed in Germany's DiGA directory doesn't guarantee revenue — prescribing and payment workflows between physicians, patients, and insurers create operational complexity. Country-specific procurement processes, tender requirements, and reimbursement criteria make commercialization an uphill battle even for technically sound products.

### Pain Point 7: Sales team scaling too early
Building a full commercial team before product-market fit is established burns cash without generating proportional revenue. The right sequence: founder-led or advisor-led early sales to validate the commercial thesis, then scale the sales team with a proven playbook.

### Pain Point 8: Misaligned incentives and missing champions
A product that benefits patients but burdens providers or finance teams will not get adopted. Incentives must be aligned across all stakeholders. A clinical champion without a financial champion is insufficient for purchasing decisions.

## 16. Key Principles Summary

1. **Start from the invoice, work backwards** — Every product decision should be traceable to a commercial requirement.
2. **Buyers ≠ users** — In healthcare, the person who benefits from the product is rarely the person who approves the purchase. Both must be served.
3. **Procurement is not the enemy** — It's a filter for completeness, compliance, and risk. Design your commercial package for it.
4. **Clinical evidence is necessary, not sufficient** — Payers and VACs need economic evidence too. Build both simultaneously.
5. **Pilots without conversion paths are waste** — Structure pilots to generate the data that triggers purchase, not just usage.
6. **Infrastructure is a product deliverable** — Training, onboarding, support, and integration are what you're actually selling alongside the device.
7. **Reimbursement must be designed in parallel with the product** — If there's no billing code or coverage pathway, clinical success is irrelevant.
8. **Impact follows the money** — Measure outcomes for whoever bears the cost or captures the benefit, not just the most obvious end user.
9. **Both markets require local expertise** — US and European markets have fundamentally different procurement, regulatory, and reimbursement structures.
10. **Scaling sales requires a playbook** — Repeatable, teachable commercial processes outperform individual heroics at scale.

Under formal distribution framework agreements, DHW takes on the following responsibilities:

The product company/manufacturer retains ownership of:

- Product-level (Tier 2/3) technical support
- Integration and EHR/RIS technical work
- Pricing authority and EULA/end-user terms

### Contracting Structure

Distribution arrangements typically follow a two-document hierarchy:
1. **Distributor Framework Agreement (DFA)**: The overarching legal and commercial terms governing the relationship — territory, responsibilities, IP, confidentiality, termination, etc.
2. **Product Terms Sheet (PTS)**: Product-specific terms covering functionality, pricing, commission models, onboarding deliverables, support tiers, and billing mechanics. Each product gets its own PTS under the same DFA.

Additional exhibits may cover territory/account registration rules, pricing schedules, and market-specific addenda (e.g., US-specific CPT code alignment, EU-specific MDR considerations).
