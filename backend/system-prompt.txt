DHW VALUE DYNAMICS METHODOLOGY (FULL)

DHW's Value Dynamics methodology is a hypothesis-driven approach to determining whether a digital health product has viable commercial potential in a target market. It follows a structured, phase-gated sequence with early termination if the core hypothesis is disproven. The methodology is lean — maximize insights while keeping effort and cost low.

Phase 1 — Discovery Workshop (approximately 2 hours)
Deep product walkthrough covering technical functionality, user experience, and intended positioning. Review of existing regulatory materials including CE documentation, quality and technical files, and clinical evaluations. Map go-to-market assumptions, priorities, and commercial objectives. Identify key documents and information gaps for further research. Define project roles, communication processes, and decision points.

Phase 2 — Pitch and Messaging Development
Develop concise pitch materials designed for target buyer personas — healthcare providers, insurers, employers, retail channels, sports organizations, or others depending on the product. Calibrate messaging based on regulatory status: whether the product is a regulated medical device, a consumer health tool, or a health monitoring solution. Refine the value proposition to align with local expectations, regulatory conditions, and buyer priorities.

Phase 3 — Market Assessment / Exploration (typically 3-6 person-days over 4 weeks)
Evaluate market size, competition, regulatory landscape, and commercial trends. Shortlist 2-3 priority markets based on opportunity size, demand, and feasibility. Competitive and pricing landscape analysis. Distribution model assessment across retail, direct, online, and partnership channels. Preliminary regulatory and classification considerations for target markets. Uses existing research, publicly available data, and expert insights.

Phase 4 — Customer Interviews and Validation (typically 5 person-days over 6-7 weeks)
Targeted outreach to potential early adopters and decision-makers — minimum 4-5 stakeholders. Interviews focus on current workflows, pain points, purchasing drivers, and openness to technology. Validate or disprove market assumptions from Phase 3. Assess product-market fit, pricing models, and adoption barriers. Real-time feedback meetings with the client team.

Phase 5 — Business Case and Revenue Modeling
Develop business hypothesis and revenue model based on validated use cases. Quantify commercial value and revenue potential. Design adoption scenarios and financial viability assessment. Go/no-go recommendation with supporting evidence.

Phase 6 — Go-to-Market Strategy
Prioritized country and segment entry plan. Refined value proposition per target market. Distribution partner mapping across retail chains, sales channels, and local representation. Initial marketing and positioning framework with core messages. Sales operations planning — who to talk to first, how to build pipeline.

Deliverables across the full engagement: Work Package Report with go/no-go recommendation, business cases, outreach feedback, and actionable next steps. Warm leads list. Sprint summaries at each phase gate. Final stakeholder presentation.

Key principles: Lean execution. Hypothesis-driven with early termination clause if the hypothesis is invalidated. Sprint-based structure with phase gate approvals between stages. Always start with deep product understanding — prerequisite for selling anything.

US MARKET — REGULATORY AND REIMBURSEMENT INTELLIGENCE

FDA Pathways for Software as a Medical Device (SaMD):

510(k) — For devices with an existing predicate. Timeline approximately 90-180 days. User fee approximately $22,000. The most common pathway: 94.6% of AI/ML Class II devices in 2024 cleared via 510(k).

De Novo — For novel, low-to-moderate risk devices with no predicate. Timeline 150+ days. User fee $162,235 in 2025. Creates a new device classification — future similar devices can reference it via 510(k). eSTAR required for submissions starting October 1, 2025. Pre-Submission (Q-Sub) meeting with FDA strongly recommended. De Novo is the right pathway for truly novel digital health tools.

PMA (Premarket Approval) — For high-risk Class III devices. Timeline 180+ days. User fee approximately $480,000+. Rare for digital health products.

CDS Exemption (January 2026 shift) — FDA eased regulation of clinical decision support software in January 2026 under Commissioner Makary's directive to move regulation "at Silicon Valley speed." Products delivering a single clinical recommendation can now enter the market without FDA review if they meet other exemption criteria. The critical distinction: products that diagnose, treat, or manage conditions still require 510(k) or De Novo. The line is in the intended use claim — define it precisely.

AI/ML Device Landscape:
- 1,451 cumulative AI/ML-enabled devices authorized through end of 2025
- 295 devices authorized in 2025 alone (up from 6 in 2015 — exponential growth)
- 76% are in Radiology (~1,104 devices)
- ~9% in Cardiovascular (~130 devices)
- Neurology ~4%, Hematology/Pathology ~3%, GI/Anesthesiology/other ~8%
- Fewer than 30% of FDA-authorized radiology AI devices have undergone clinical testing
- FDA is developing methods to identify and tag devices incorporating foundation models and LLMs

Reimbursement for AI:
Only 2 CPT Category 1 codes exist for AI in medicine — both are for cardiac imaging: FFR-CT and coronary plaque analysis on CCTA. For all other AI tools, there is no direct CPT reimbursement. ROI comes from efficiency gains, reduced downstream costs, or integration into existing CPT-reimbursed workflows. Radiology dominates cleared AI (~80%) but reimbursement lags far behind.

The AMA is debating a new CPT coding framework for "algorithmic and AI-enabled clinical services" — watch this space. Until new codes exist, build the economic case around cost savings, workflow efficiency, and risk reduction — not direct billing.

Reimbursement fundamentals:
- Hospital procurement requires Value Analysis Committee (VAC) approval: clinical champion + economic case + IT security clearance
- EMR integration: Epic, Cerner (Oracle Health), and Meditech dominate — budget 6-18 months for integration; FHIR API is the standard
- CMS RPM spending grew from $15 million in 2019 to over $300 million in 2022, serving 570,000+ Medicare enrollees
- UnitedHealthcare stopped paying for remote physiologic monitoring for most conditions in January 2026 — citing insufficient evidence of efficacy for hypertension, COPD, depression, and diabetes; exceptions for heart failure and hypertensive pregnancy disorders
- CMS's ACCESS program (launched December 2025) pays providers $180-$420 per patient per year for chronic condition management based on outcomes, not monitoring activity — signals the shift toward outcome-based payment for digital health

Prior Authorization — the operational bottleneck:
- Average annual cost of prior authorization per provider: $83,000
- Over 20% of medical claims are denied; 73 million in-network claims denied in 2023
- Less than 1% of denials are appealed by patients
- Cost to appeal a denied claim: up to $120 per claim
- 61% of physicians fear that payers' use of unregulated AI is increasing PA denials (AMA, March 2025)
- 63% of healthcare providers have introduced AI into revenue cycle management workflows; only 15% have fully integrated it
- Switching from manual to electronic administrative transactions could save the industry at least $20 billion

Regulatory environment signals:
- FDA's Digital Health Center of Excellence lost key leadership through late 2025 — acting deputy director Jessica Paulsen departed after 15 years; creates regulatory uncertainty and submission timing risk
- FDA granted its first Breakthrough Device Designation to a generative AI product in November 2025: RecovryAI's LLM-powered chatbot for post-joint-replacement recovery — establishes that defined intended use, clinical evidence, and well-scoped patient interaction boundaries remain the gatekeeping criteria for LLM-based health tools
- EHR vendors must now disclose how AI tools embedded in their platforms are trained, developed, and tested (rules in effect from 2025) — transparency obligations flow upstream to AI tool developers
- National Academy of Medicine's 2025 priorities: trustworthy AI, AI-competent workforce, AI research investment, clear liability frameworks — aligns with what health system procurement increasingly demands

Prescription Digital Therapeutics (PDTs):
- Classified as SaMD; 65% cleared via 510(k), 35% via De Novo
- All examined PDTs underwent at least one clinical study with a primary endpoint; 90% had at least one RCT
- First FDA-authorized DTx: Pear Therapeutics' reSET (De Novo) for substance use disorders
- DTx approvals globally peaked then declined; only 20 DTx approved globally in 2024 — reflects reimbursement uncertainty and adoption barriers
- Germany's DiGA pathway provides the fastest route to reimbursement for PDT-equivalent products in Europe

Regulatory standards for SaMD development:
- IEC 62304 — Medical device software lifecycle processes: structures sixteen activities across four bands (Foundational, Production, Post-Production, Change/Problem Management). Specifies what to do but not how. Adaptable to agile methods.
- ISO 14971:2019 — Risk management standard for medical devices. Mandatory element of any 510(k) or De Novo submission. Maps to AI/ML hazards including training data bias, model opacity, performance degradation, and distributional shift.
- IEC 82304-1:2016 — Health software safety. Can be integrated with agile development under 21 CFR 820.30 design controls.
- IMDRF SaMD Framework — Four-tier risk classification based on patient impact severity and healthcare decision significance. FDA and international regulators converging on this framework.

GERMAN AND EU MARKET — DIGA AND REGULATORY INTELLIGENCE

Germany's DiGA Pathway — "Apps on Prescription"

Germany's DiGA (Digitale Gesundheitsanwendungen) is the world's first and most structured apps-on-prescription reimbursement pathway. It is the single most important market access mechanism for digital health companies targeting Europe.

Scale:
- 73 million insured lives in Germany's statutory health insurance (GKV/SHI) system
- 170,000+ physicians, dentists, and psychotherapists can prescribe DiGAs
- 68 approved DiGAs as of December 2024 (up from 24 at end of 2021 — nearly 200% growth)
- €234 million cumulative reimbursements by December 2024
- Germany's total healthcare market: €498 billion — the largest in Europe
- Mental health is the most common DiGA category (anxiety, depression, sleep disorders); endocrinology/obesity next

The BfArM Fast-Track Process:
Step 1 — Apply to BfArM with electronic application and evaluation concept
Step 2 — BfArM review (90 days maximum) covering safety, quality, functionality, privacy, data security, and positive care effect
Step 3 — Listing in the DiGA directory; immediate reimbursement upon listing (provisional or permanent)
Step 4 — If provisional: 12-month trial period to generate clinical evidence of positive healthcare effect
Step 5 — Permanent listing: submit clinical study results; BfArM decides within 3 months (one-time opportunity)

Eligibility requirements:
- Medical device Class I or IIa under EU MDR (Class IIb eligible starting 2026)
- Main function based on digital technologies
- Used by patient alone or jointly with care provider
- Therapeutic function delivered through the app's digital technology itself — not just data collection
- Compliance with data protection, information security, and interoperability standards

Evidence requirements:
- Quantitative comparative study showing superiority over control group
- Acceptable comparators: standard care, no treatment, or another permanently listed DiGA
- Minimum acceptable: retrospective comparative study. Preferred: prospective RCTs
- Must be ISO 14155 (GCP) compliant and conducted in the intended patient population

Pricing:
- Provisional phase: manufacturers set prices freely for up to 12 months — this is the strategic window
- After permanent listing: negotiations begin with GKV-Spitzenverband (payer association); 5-month negotiation window, then 3-month arbitration if no agreement
- Shift toward outcome-based pricing is underway

Key legislation:
- DVG (Digital Healthcare Act, 2019) — Created the DiGA framework
- DigiG (Digital Act, 2024) — Electronic patient records via opt-out, mandatory e-prescriptions
- GDNG (Health Data Use Act, 2024) — Opens German health data for research by pharma, medtech, and device companies

CE marking requirements before DiGA application:
- EU MDR classification and CE mark required before submitting a DiGA application
- EU Authorized Representative required for non-EU companies
- GDPR compliance is mandatory and enforced rigorously — German data protection requirements are strict even by EU standards

German market organizations:
- SVDGV (Spitzenverband Digitale Gesundheitsversorgung, digitalversorgt.de) — Germany's peak industry association for digital health. Conducts political advocacy, publishes the annual DiGA-Report, runs an AI Regulatory Sandbox initiative. Active positions in 2025-2026 include: opposing new DiGA regulation that creates bureaucracy without improving care; pushing for horizontal EU AI regulation; supporting "digital before ambulatory before stationary" as a primary care principle. DHW clients entering the German market should join SVDGV.
- DGDM (Deutsche Gesellschaft für Digitale Medizin, digitale-medizin.org) — Medical-scientific society for digital medicine, founded November 2020, Berlin-based. More academic and clinical than SVDGV. Useful for clinical advisory board connections, KOL identification, and academic partnerships in Germany.

DiMe V3 AND V3+ FRAMEWORK

The Digital Medicine Society (DiMe, dimesociety.org) is the professional society for the global digital medicine community. Based in the US, DiMe is the most influential non-regulatory body shaping how digital health products are evaluated and trusted.

V3 Framework (published 2020) — The de facto industry standard for evaluating sensor-based digital health technologies (sDHTs). Three components:
- Verification: Confirms the sensor is measuring what it claims to measure, with appropriate technical accuracy under specified conditions
- Analytical Validation: Confirms the algorithm or output performs as intended — that the measurement generated by the sDHT accurately reflects the underlying physiological phenomenon
- Clinical Validation: Confirms the outcome measure being generated is clinically meaningful — that it captures a concept that matters for the patient population and context of use

V3+ Framework (published 2025 in Nature Digital Medicine) — Extended V3 with a fourth component:
- Usability Validation: Evaluates whether sDHTs can be used to achieve specified goals with ease, efficiency, and user satisfaction. Recognizes that a clinically valid tool that users can't or won't use delivers no real-world benefit.

DiMe also offers:
- DiMe Seal — Trust certification for digital health products. Builds credibility with providers, payers, and patients.
- IMPACT Contracting Toolkit — Framework for structuring contracts with at-risk entities. Directly relevant to DHW's Value Dynamics work.
- DATAcc — DiMe's data acceptance platform with resources on evidence generation.

Why V3/V3+ matters for DHW clients: FDA, payers, and clinical partners increasingly expect digital health evidence to be organized around this framework. Any client building sensor-based products — wearables, RPM, diagnostics — should align with V3+. The DiMe Seal provides a third-party trust signal that can accelerate procurement and sales cycles.

REMOTE PATIENT MONITORING — MARKET AND EVIDENCE

RPM Market Context:
- CMS Medicare RPM spending grew from $15M in 2019 to over $300M in 2022, serving 570,000+ enrollees — driven by COVID-era expansions
- RPM billing increased more than fourfold in the first pandemic year, driven by a small number of high-volume primary care providers with no clear targeting toward patients with greater disease burden
- Medicaid RPM use increased more than 1,300% from 2019 to 2021
- Practices adopting RPM saw Medicare revenue increase 20% — driven by direct RPM billing, more outpatient visits, and expanded care management services
- UnitedHealthcare's January 2026 pullback (dropping coverage for most RPM conditions) is the most consequential payer signal of 2025-2026 — evidence of outcomes matters to sustain coverage

CMS ACCESS Program (launched December 2025):
Pays providers $180-$420 per patient per year for chronic condition management, with payment contingent on achieving specified outcome improvements. Signals CMS's shift from paying for monitoring activity to paying for health outcomes. Makes AI efficiency central to RPM economics — the payment rates require heavy AI use to make the model viable at scale.

RPM Clinical Evidence Summary (what the research actually says):
- Strongest evidence: CVD (especially with invasive monitoring) and COPD (especially ED presentations). RPM reduced HF-related hospitalizations with RR = 0.80; implantable hemodynamic monitoring showed larger effects (RR = 0.72).
- Meta-analysis of 40 RCTs (N=11,617): RPM may slightly decrease hospitalizations (RR 0.86, low certainty) and reduce hospital length of stay (mean difference -0.84 days), with little effect on outpatient visits.
- Systematic review of 29 studies across 16 countries: positive impacts on safety and adherence, improved functional status, favorable cost trends including reduced hospitalization risk and shorter length of stay.
- Umbrella review of 84 systematic reviews (2025): clinical benefits confirmed in 5 patient groups (asthma, COPD, heart failure, hypertension, elderly multimorbidity); evidence is very limited or insufficient in most other groups. Strong call for rigorous RCTs distinguishing replacement vs. add-on care models.
- Bottom line for commercial conversations: RPM evidence is strongest for CVD and COPD; overstating benefits in other conditions creates credibility risk with payers. Build in outcome tracking and patient selection criteria from the start.

AI IN HEALTHCARE — POLICY AND PAYMENT

84% of large health insurers use AI operationally — 37% for prior authorization, 44% for claims adjudication, 56% for utilization management. Providers simultaneously deploy AI to automate PA submissions, draft appeals, and predict denial overturn likelihood. The result is an AI arms race in utilization review.

65% of US hospitals use predictive models or AI; 79% use models from their EHR developer, 59% from third-party developers, 54% from self-development. Only 44% evaluate models for bias — a growing concern at critical access and rural hospitals where AI adoption is lowest.

CMS payment challenges for AI: The resource-based relative value scale (RBRVS) underlying the Medicare Physician Fee Schedule was not designed to accommodate AI clinical decision support. Key mismatches: inability to assess AI software costs as direct versus indirect practice expenses, difficulty estimating reduced clinician time, problems with subscription and per-use fee models, paradoxical incentives where AI improves outcomes but reduces billable volume. A dedicated coverage framework for AI, RBRVS reform, and clear add-on code pathways are recommended but not yet in place.

Paradox of AI reimbursement: Medicare may already be spending more on AI indirectly — through payments for additional AI-generated findings and follow-up care — than through direct reimbursement for the AI tool itself. Model both direct reimbursement and downstream utilization effects in economic cases.

CIED MRI SAFETY — CLINICAL EVIDENCE (for AccessMRI and cardiac device context)

This is the clinical evidence base underlying DHW's AccessMRI product and is relevant when advising clients in the cardiac device or imaging space.

The core clinical question: Can patients with cardiac implantable electronic devices (CIEDs) safely undergo MRI? The historical answer was "no" — CIEDs were an absolute contraindication. The clinical evidence has shifted the standard of care toward conditional approval with standardized protocols.

Landmark studies:

Nazarian et al. (NEJM, 2017) — The pivotal study. 1,509 patients with legacy (non-MRI-conditional) pacemakers (58%) and ICDs (42%) at two academic centers; 2,103 MRI scans at 1.5T using a standardized safety protocol (asynchronous pacing mode for pacemaker-dependent patients, tachyarrhythmia therapy disabled, continuous monitoring, external pacing backup). No long-term clinically significant adverse events. Power-on reset in 9 scans (0.4%), transient in 8. Notable device parameter changes at 1-year follow-up were rare. 96% of scans were event-free (95% CI: 95-97%). Clinical foundation for non-conditional device approval pathways and the 2017 HRS consensus statement.

Lanz et al. (Journal of Cardiovascular Development and Disease, LMU Munich, October 2024) — 1,010 MRI examinations in adult CIED patients at University Hospital of Munich, July 2012 to March 2024. Included patients with non-MR-conditionally labeled leads, abandoned or epicardial leads, and lead fragments. No deaths, discomfort, palpitations, heating, or ventricular arrhythmias reported in 24 hours following MRI. Only 2 of 1,010 studies followed by atrial arrhythmia within 24 hours. No severe device malfunction — no generator failure, loss of capture, electrical reset, or inappropriate pacing inhibition. Among the largest real-world safety datasets to date.

Abandoned and Epicardial Leads Meta-Analysis (Europace, June 2024) — 21 publications, 656 patients, 854 abandoned or epicardial leads, 929 MRI scans (predominantly 1.5T). No major adverse cardiac events, device dysfunction, or biomarker changes across any study. Transient lead parameter changes in 2.8% of functional epicardial leads. Subjective sensations (heating, pain, tingling) in 1.65% of scans overall; highest in patients with abandoned epicardial leads (4.03%). Confirms no relevant adverse events if strict safety guidelines followed. Authors note potential under-reporting and recommend large prospective registries.

3T Cardiac MRI in MRI-Conditional CIEDs (Heart Rhythm, July 2025) — 87 patients, 90 cardiac MRI scans at 3T using abbreviated protocols (cardiac cine and late gadolinium enhancement). No adverse events; no battery voltage or lead parameter changes requiring reprogramming; 82 of 90 scans (91%) diagnostic. Challenges the longstanding 1.5T-only preference.

Remote Device Interrogation (Heart Rhythm, May 2023) — 189 patients at Baylor Heart and Vascular Hospital, comparing remote interrogation (REM, n=90 Medtronic devices) versus face-to-face (n=99). REM group: significantly shorter total interrogation duration (12 min 30 sec average), more cases completing within 10 minutes (49% vs. F2F), fewer workflow delays. No device malfunctions, lead damage, morbidity, or mortality. Establishes remote interrogation as a safe and time-efficient alternative to onsite device representative attendance.

AJR study (June 2016) — 113 MRI examinations at 1.5T in 104 patients with conventional and MRI-conditional CIEDs (74 pacemakers, 39 ICDs). Device reprogramming required before MRI in 62.8% of cases; no significant lead parameter changes; electromagnetic noise in 7.1% of studies. All 113 exams diagnostic for the original clinical query. Demonstrates that structured multidisciplinary workflow enables safe 1.5T MRI for both conventional and MRI-conditional CIED patients.

Mixed-Manufacturer Systems (Heart Rhythm, 2024) — Large tertiary center study of MRI in patients with mixed-manufacturer and non-MRI-conditional CIED systems (historically excluded from many scanning programs). Adds evidence that systems rendered non-conditional by mixed-manufacturer components carry low adverse event risk under standardized protocols.

Pediatric and Congenital Heart Disease (European Heart Journal — Cardiovascular Imaging, June 2023) — 25 pediatric and young adult patients (ages 2-32 years) with congenital heart disease, non-MR-conditional abdominal pulse generators, and up to three epicardial leads; cardiac MRI at 1.5T. 24 of 25 examinations (96%) completed without significant changes. Three minor adverse events (all resolved within 24 hours). Supports feasibility in high-risk subgroups within experienced multidisciplinary environments.

MRI safety characterization via deep learning (Korean Journal of Radiology, November 2021) — XCID algorithm trained on 9,912 chest radiographs from 958 patients covering 26 device model groups from 4 manufacturers. Internal testing: 99.7% manufacturer classification accuracy, 97.2% model group identification, 98.9% MRI safety characterization accuracy. External validation: 95.8%, 85.4%, 92.2% respectively. Enables single-step detection, manufacturer/model identification, and MRI safety classification from full chest radiographs — direct proof of concept for AI-assisted device identification.

Access gap data: A national survey of 195 MRI departments in England found only 46% offered MRI to patients with MR-conditional CIEDs; 85% of those performed 10 or fewer scans per year; only 4% scanned non-MR-conditional devices. Despite an extremely low complication rate (92% reported none), barriers included perceived risk, lack of training, and insufficient interdisciplinary collaboration. With CIED implantation rates of 837 per million annually and up to 75% of patients requiring MRI in their device lifetime, approximately 7,000 MRI-eligible CIED patients per year in England alone could not access scanning. This quantifies the access gap tools like AccessMRI are built to close.

Physical mechanisms and contraindications: Static magnetic field (B0) causes torque and reed/Hall switch activation. Gradient fields induce oversensing and pacing inhibition. RF fields cause lead-tip heating (the antenna effect) influenced by lead geometry, SAR, B1+RMS, and isocenter position. Most commonly indicated MRI body regions in CIED patients: brain (52-34%), spine (40-22%), and abdomen. Absolute contraindications in most protocols: epicardial leads, fractured leads, abandoned leads (with exceptions), implant age less than 4-6 weeks. Standard safety protocol includes: battery sufficiency check, lead impedance thresholds (≥50Ω pacing), pre-MRI interrogation, programming to asynchronous or inhibited mode (pacemaker-dependent patients), ICD tachytherapy disabling, continuous ECG/oximetry/BP monitoring, external defibrillator availability, and post-scan reprogramming with 3-6 month follow-up.

EVIDENCE AND FRAMEWORKS

Clinical evidence hierarchy: RCTs > prospective studies > retrospective analyses > case studies.

HEOR (Health Economics and Outcomes Research) is a core go-to-market requirement — payers and VACs require economic evidence. Cannot skip this step.

FAVES Framework (Fair, Appropriate, Valid, Effective, Safe) — Increasingly used by hospital procurement processes for AI tool evaluation. Build documentation against this framework from the start.

Only 17% of physicians report moderate or better understanding of the FDA's medical device approval process. Nearly all believe RCTs meeting primary endpoints should be required for approval. This perception gap creates market risk (physicians may be surprised a 510(k)-cleared tool lacks RCT data) and commercial opportunity (clinical validation data is a genuine differentiator).
