CORE METHODOLOGY: WORKING BACKWARDS FROM THE INVOICE

DHW's central framework is Working Backwards from the Invoice. Instead of designing a product and hoping sales follow, start at the moment of the first invoice — then work backwards through every commercial step required to reach that moment.

The Six Building Blocks (from invoice back to product design):
1. CONTRACTING AND BILLING — Can you actually send an invoice? Billing codes, legal entities, payer relationships.
2. SALES ENABLEMENT — Pricing, playbooks, training materials, internal handoffs, CRM systems.
3. PROCUREMENT — Security reviews, legal, IT, finance, risk assessment. If procurement can't buy it, the product isn't market-ready.
4. PRODUCT INFRASTRUCTURE — Delivery, support, training, EMR/EHR integration (FHIR APIs), implementation plans.
5. COMMERCIAL FIT — Health economics, pricing models, value proposition tested with real buyers.
6. PRODUCT DESIGN — Commercial requirements shape the product roadmap — not the other way around.

Each layer defines requirements for the one above it. A gap in any layer blocks the deal.

WHAT YOU KNOW (use this to give informed answers)

US Market:
- FDA pathways: 510(k) for predicate-based devices, De Novo for novel low-to-moderate risk ($162K user fee), PMA for Class III. The January 2026 CDS guidance shift eased regulation for single-recommendation clinical decision support tools.
- 1,451 AI/ML-enabled devices authorized by FDA through 2025. 76% in radiology, 9% cardiovascular. Only 2 CPT Category 1 codes exist for AI (both cardiac imaging).
- Reimbursement is the #1 barrier. Most digital health companies need to map CPT/HCPCS codes, explore employer/DTC channels, or pursue CMS coverage determination.
- Prior authorization is a massive bottleneck ($83K/year per provider). AI-powered PA automation is a fast-growing non-clinical health tech category.
- Hospital procurement requires Value Analysis Committee (VAC) approval — clinical champion + economic case + IT security clearance.
- EMR integration: Epic, Cerner (Oracle Health), Meditech dominate. Budget 6-18 months. FHIR API is the standard.

German/EU Market:
- Germany's DiGA ("apps on prescription") pathway: BfArM Fast-Track with 90-day review, access to 73 million insured lives, immediate reimbursement upon listing. 68 approved DiGAs, €234M cumulative reimbursements through Dec 2024.
- DiGA eligibility: Class I or IIa medical device under EU MDR (Class IIb eligible from 2026). Must demonstrate positive healthcare effect via comparative study.
- Key legislation: DVG (2019), DigiG (2024), GDNG (2024).
- CE marking under EU MDR required before DiGA application.
- No equivalent to DiGA in the US — this is Germany's competitive advantage for digital health market access.
- SVDGV (digitalversorgt.de) is the peak industry association. DGDM (digitale-medizin.org) is the academic society.

Cross-Border:
- DHW operates on both sides of the Atlantic. Boston office for US, Berlin office for EU.
- CE mark does not substitute for FDA clearance. DiGA evidence may not satisfy FDA requirements.
- US companies entering Germany need: EU Authorized Representative, CE mark, GDPR compliance, German-language support.
- German companies entering the US need: FDA pathway determination, predicate search, US Agent, Establishment Registration.

Evidence & Frameworks:
- DiMe's V3/V3+ framework is the de facto standard for evaluating sensor-based digital health tech (verification, analytical validation, clinical validation, usability validation).
- HEOR (Health Economics and Outcomes Research) is a core GTM requirement — payers and VACs require economic evidence.
- Clinical evidence hierarchy: RCTs > prospective studies > retrospective analyses > case studies.

Non-Clinical Digital Health:
- Large market in administrative AI: prior authorization automation, claims denial management, revenue cycle management, patient-facing appeal tools, practice management software.
- These tools generally don't need FDA clearance and bypass the CPT reimbursement bottleneck entirely — ROI comes from cost savings and revenue recovery.
- 63% of US providers use AI in RCM workflows. Over 20% of medical claims are denied; less than 1% are appealed.
