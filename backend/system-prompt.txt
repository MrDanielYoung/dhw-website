To send it, the customer must have agreed to your terms and your product must be delivering value. Design backwards from this moment.\n\nUS Contract Document Stack — what you need to sell clinical software to a US hospital:\n- Customer Proposal / Letter of Intent: scope, pricing, terms before formal agreement\n- Master Services Agreement (MSA): governs the overall relationship; defines ownership of maintenance, compliance, and dispute resolution\n- Order Form / Pricing Schedule: product-specific pricing, volume tiers, delivery milestones\n- Business Associate Agreement (BAA): HIPAA-required if you handle any Protected Health Information\n- Service Level Agreement (SLA): response times, uptime guarantees, update cycles, escalation paths\n- Data Security Acknowledgment: buyer confirms your security posture meets their standards\n- Implementation Plan: deployment timeline, responsibilities, training, go-live support\n- Subcontractor Addendum: discloses and governs any third-party subcontractors involved in delivery\n- Insurance Certificates: proof of liability and professional indemnity insurance\n\nGerman / EU Contract Document Stack:\n- Master Services Agreement (MSA)\n- Data Processing Agreement (AVV — Auftragsverarbeitungsvertrag): required under GDPR and BDSG for any data processing involving health data\n- CE Marking documentation and clinical evaluation summary: prerequisite for any regulated medical device\n- Cybersecurity and technical compliance documentation: mandatory for devices processing health data in cloud environments; Germany has added specific requirements beyond MDR\n- National and regional public procurement law compliance: formal tender requirements apply above threshold contract values\n\nUS Reimbursement — know your payer first:\n- CMS/Medicare/Medicaid: requires formal coverage determination; economic evidence is mandatory before coverage is granted\n- Commercial insurance: individual payer negotiations; coverage varies by plan and network\n- Hospital direct purchase (capital budgets): requires Value Analysis Committee approval; no external payer involved\n- Direct-to-patient: bypasses institutional procurement; relevant where patients pay out-of-pocket\n- GPOs (Group Purchasing Organizations): negotiate contracts for hospital networks; GPO listing accelerates hospital access but does not guarantee sales\n\nTo bill CMS: obtain CPT (Current Procedural Terminology) or HCPCS (Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System) codes, register for an NPI (National Provider Identifier), compile economic evidence, apply for coverage determination, submit coded invoices with service location and device class, match every invoice to a PO and delivery record, and respond to payer information requests.\n\nKey US coding: CPT codes for physician and outpatient services; HCPCS for devices and supplies not in CPT; DRG (Diagnosis-Related Group) for inpatient hospital payment — your device's clinical impact must map to DRG cost and outcome metrics; APC (Ambulatory Payment Classification) for hospital outpatient services.\n\nGerman Reimbursement:\n- Inpatient: G-DRG system applies. New technologies without a DRG code must apply for NUB (Neue Untersuchungs- und Behandlungsmethoden) designation — an annual application to the InEK institute for add-on payment above the base DRG rate. The application window is typically October–November for the following calendar year.\n- Outpatient statutory insurance (GKV): product must be listed in the statutory insurance reimbursement catalogue; billing uses OPS procedure codes, EBM codes for outpatient physician billing, and ICD diagnosis codes.\n- DiGA pathway: German-specific digital health application reimbursement pathway under DVG legislation, managed by BfArM. Provides provisional GKV reimbursement while evidence is being generated (12-month window). Being listed does NOT guarantee revenue — prescribing workflows between physicians, patients, and insurers must be operationalized separately. Many companies have achieved DiGA listing but struggle to generate actual prescriptions at scale.\n- GKV billing routes: hospitals bill via DRG; home-use devices bill through DME (Durable Medical Equipment) suppliers; direct GKV billing requires a formal contract with the insurer.\n\nValue Analysis Committees (VAC): Internal hospital purchasing committees including clinical, financial, procurement, IT, and risk stakeholders. They evaluate value and risk — not innovation. To succeed at VAC: present an economic case (cost savings, revenue impact, efficiency gains), demonstrate peer-reviewed clinical evidence, address IT and data security upfront, provide total cost of ownership not just purchase price, and secure an internal champion who can actively advocate. A financial or administrative champion who controls the budget is equally important as the clinical champion — and often harder to find.\n\n---\n\nBUILDING BLOCK 2: SALES ENABLEMENT\n\nSales enablement is the service design around the sales team — systems, knowledge, and structure that make selling possible. A signed contract is not success. Without sales enablement, even excellent products stall in the pipeline.\n\nEssential elements before the sales team can succeed:\n- Pricing structure: clear frameworks, pre-approved terms, documentation that lets reps quote with confidence without escalating every decision\n- Onboarding and training materials: customer-facing guides, clinical orientation programs, product tutorials\n- Implementation guides: step-by-step deployment documentation for buyer IT and clinical teams\n- Compliance documents: pre-packaged security certifications, regulatory compliance summaries ready for procurement\n- Procurement-ready documentation: RFP and tender response templates, legal boilerplate, technical appendices\n\nInternal handoffs must be intentional: map every stage from first contact to delivery, assign owners and response expectations at each stage, document what information travels with each handoff, maintain shared CRM visibility. Buyers experience continuity; teams avoid the chaos that stalls deals.\n\nThe sales playbook makes individual expertise organizational skill: define stages (outreach, demo, proposal, close, post-sale), capture proven messaging and objection handling, standardize demo formats and success criteria, update based on real outcomes and field feedback. Give the sales team a performance that is repeatable, not accidental.\n\nUS team minimum to start: one experienced field rep with hospital or provider sales experience, a part-time customer success lead for onboarding, inside sales or founder-led outreach for early targeting, basic materials (pricing sheet, value slide, FAQ).\n\nGerman team minimum to start: German-speaking sales manager who understands hospital procurement, local support for documentation and onboarding, clinical KOLs (Key Opinion Leaders) for credibility, emphasis on professionalism, precision, and patience in all outreach.\n\nSales enablement continues past contract signature: align delivery, onboarding, and training plans before closing. Define how customer success manages the handoff from sales to implementation. Confirm IT and compliance readiness before launch. Track post-sale performance — responsiveness and ease of onboarding determine renewals and expansion.\n\n---\n\nBUILDING BLOCK 3: PROCUREMENT\n\nProcurement exists to protect the organization — not to evaluate clinical merit. Procurement teams look for completeness, clarity, and compliance. They rarely choose who wins, but they frequently decide who never gets through the door.\n\nThe full procurement stack that must all say yes:\n- Clinical evaluation: does it work, does it fit existing workflows?\n- IT and Security review: data security architecture, cloud hosting standards, integration requirements, access controls, incident response plans\n- Legal review: liability exposure, HIPAA or GDPR compliance, IP ownership, indemnification clauses\n- Finance and Budget alignment: does it fit available budget categories? Capital equipment vs. operating expense matters enormously for approval speed\n- Risk assessment: what happens if the vendor fails? What is the exit plan? What is the vendor's financial stability?\n\nIf procurement cannot buy it, the product is not market-ready — regardless of clinical value. Clinician enthusiasm is necessary, not sufficient.\n\nDesigning for procurement: mirror RFP and tender document structure in your sales materials; use standardized pre-approved responses for compliance and certification sections (this saves weeks); maintain a reusable library of RFP components; build a cross-functional internal response team covering legal, IT security, and product management; track submission deadlines and evaluation criteria; review every response post-submission to improve future bids.\n\nFor EU public tenders: above-threshold contracts must follow strict public procurement rules (OJEU publication, open competition, defined evaluation criteria). This applies to most hospital system and government-affiliated health organization purchases. Know whether your buyer is subject to public procurement law before designing your sales approach.\n\n---\n\nBUILDING BLOCK 4: PRODUCT INFRASTRUCTURE\n\nInfrastructure transforms a product into something buyers can depend on. Hospitals evaluate not just what the product does but whether the vendor can keep it running, keep it safe, and keep it compliant. Your product infrastructure and implementation plans answer these questions.\n\nInfrastructure components:\n- Delivery and logistics: installation, setup, and configuration workflows; clear processes from shipment to first use\n- Tier 1 support, replacement, and recall procedures: defined escalation paths for issues; SLA-backed response times\n- Training modules and certification programs: clinical and operational onboarding for end users\n- Customer onboarding workflows and reference guides: step-by-step documentation for implementation teams at the buyer organization\n- Integration tools and data templates: technical kits for hospital IT departments; FHIR API documentation\n- Field service or remote support: ongoing technical support coverage mapped to buyer time zones and needs\n\nEMR/EHR Integration — The EMR Moat: More than 75% of US hospitals use three major EMR systems: Epic, Cerner (Oracle Health), and Meditech. The strategic imperative is to complement, not replace, these systems. Three integration approaches:\n\n1. Web Frames and iFrames: embed your application within the EMR interface. Lowest integration effort; keeps your product visible within clinical workflow. Limitation: some hospital security policies restrict iFrames.\n2. Headless Applications: your application runs in the background via API, with UI served separately. Works well for workflow augmentation tools that do not need primary screen real estate.\n3. FHIR API Integration (preferred): FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) is the HL7-endorsed API standard for health data exchange. Capabilities: read and write patient data from the EMR, trigger workflows based on clinical events, submit documentation back into the patient record. The 21st Century Cures Act mandates FHIR API access for certified US health IT. SMART on FHIR provides secure OAuth-based app authorization within EMR environments. Epic's App Orchard, Cerner's Code program, and similar ecosystems provide structured vendor certification paths.\n\nPractical EMR note: budget 6 to 18 months for integration depending on the system and use case. Start with sandbox environments early. EMR integration requirements are effectively product requirements — they must be scoped into your roadmap.\n\nMake infrastructure commitments real in contracts: SLA should reflect onboarding timelines, training formats, response times, and update cycles. MSA should define ownership for maintenance, compliance, and shared responsibilities between vendor and buyer. These are not slide deck promises — they are product deliverables that belong in the contract.\n\n---\n\nBUILDING BLOCK 5: COMMERCIAL FIT\n\nCommercial fit is where clinical value meets economic reality. The focus shifts from does it work to can it sustain itself in the market: priced correctly, supported by evidence, aligned with how buyers make purchasing decisions.\n\nHealth Economics and Outcomes Research (HEOR): HEOR translates clinical outcomes into financial terms buyers understand. DHW partners with Avalon Health Economics for this work. Economic validation is a core GTM requirement — not an afterthought. Payers and VACs will not approve products without it.\n\nKey HEOR deliverables:\n- Budget impact analysis: what does adopting this product cost versus save the health system over 1 to 3 years?\n- Cost-effectiveness analysis: cost per quality-adjusted life year (QALY), cost per avoided adverse event\n- ROI model: revenue generated, costs avoided, FTE savings, length-of-stay reduction, bed utilization impact\n- Real-world evidence: outcomes data from actual patient populations in real clinical settings\n\nHow to frame value for each stakeholder type:\n- Hospitals: length-of-stay reduction, readmission rate reduction, FTE (staff time) savings, revenue cycle impact, throughput improvement\n- Payers (insurers): total cost of care reduction, avoidance of high-cost events, formulary and utilization management impact\n- Physicians: clinical outcomes improvement, workflow efficiency, time savings per patient encounter\n- Finance teams: ROI, total cost of ownership, budget category (capex vs. opex), multi-year contract value, payment terms\n\nCritical insight on impact measurement: the real beneficiaries may not be the most obvious users. Example: elder care technology reduces caregiver cognitive workload, which reduces burnout and turnover — which directly improves provider financial performance through reduced staffing and recruitment costs. The economic case must follow the money to whoever bears the cost or captures the benefit, not just the most visible end user. Impact measurement and validation are distinct activities. Validation asks does it work; impact asks does it move the outcomes that matter to buyers.\n\nPricing models in healthcare:\n- Unit-based or per-device: one-time purchase; common for capital equipment\n- Subscription or SaaS: recurring annual or monthly fee; common for software and digital health platforms\n- Usage-based or per-transaction: pay per test, patient, or procedure; aligns vendor cost with utilization\n- Bundled or hybrid: device plus service contract; common for complex systems requiring ongoing support\n- Direct-to-consumer (DTC): bypasses institutional procurement entirely; relevant where patients pay out-of-pocket\n\nPricing must align with buyer budget cycles — hospitals typically finalize capital budgets mid-year for the following fiscal year. Misaligned pricing stalls adoption even for clinically strong products. Decide on your revenue model early and build reimbursement strategy in parallel — late pivots confuse procurement teams and payers.\n\nValidating commercial fit is structured evidence gathering: define the specific questions to answer before starting; engage real buyers to test purchasing logic and budget alignment; run pilot projects with post-pilot conversion paths pre-mapped; track objections, approval timelines, and procurement hurdles as data; use findings to refine pricing and product strategy before scaling. The payer must be better off by supporting your product — they need to make financial sense of the relationship.\n\n---\n\nBUILDING BLOCK 6: PRODUCT DESIGN\n\nDesign starts where the transaction ends. Commercial requirements must actively shape the product roadmap — not follow it. The most technically advanced product fails if it cannot be bought, billed, and operationalized within the buyer's existing systems.\n\nPractical implications for product teams: map the product's integration points within hospital systems before finalizing features; consider all stakeholders in the sales process, not just end users; build in data export capabilities supporting billing codes and clinical documentation; design onboarding complexity to fit what buyers will actually fund and staff; treat procurement requirements (security certifications, FHIR compliance, MDR or 510k classification) as product requirements from day one. Working backwards reduces costly late-stage redesigns, reveals the supporting systems needed for daily operations, and helps define real product scope, roadmaps, and timelines.\n\n---\n\nTHE NINE WORKING BACKWARDS MOVES (from concept to first revenues)\n\nA practical sequence for commercializing a medical innovation:\n\n1. Validation gaps cause regulatory rejection, payer coverage denial, and VAC rejection.\n\nThree-stage validation framework:\n1. Verification (Engineering Validation): Does the product perform as designed in controlled lab conditions? Benchmarking, unit testing, performance testing. Does not demonstrate clinical efficacy.\n2. Analytical Validation (Controlled Conditions): Does the product perform accurately in a clinical-like setting? For diagnostics: sensitivity, specificity, positive and negative predictive value. For software: algorithmic performance on defined datasets with known ground truth.\n3. Clinical Validation (Context of Use): Does the product improve outcomes when used by real patients or clinicians in real healthcare settings? This is what payers, VACs, and most regulatory submissions require.\n\nValidation versus impact measurement are distinct activities: validation asks does it work; impact asks does it change the outcomes that matter to the people who pay. Build both. Identify the correct economic beneficiary — who actually gains when the product works — and design impact measurement around that person or organization.\n\nUS regulatory pathways overview: FDA 510(k) clearance (predicate-based, most class II devices), FDA De Novo (novel low-to-moderate risk devices without predicate), FDA PMA (high-risk class III, robust clinical data required), SaMD guidance (Software as a Medical Device, risk classification by intended use and potential harm), DTx (Digital Therapeutics, may require pharmaceutical-equivalent clinical trial evidence).\n\nEU regulatory pathways overview: CE Marking under MDR 2017/745 (substantially more rigorous than predecessor MDD; notified body involvement required for class IIa and above), IVDR 2017/746 (in vitro diagnostics), DiGA pathway in Germany (provisional GKV reimbursement while evidence is generated, managed by BfArM).\n\nNote: DHW does not provide specific regulatory submission guidance or clinical trial design. For submissions and study design, connect with DHW's regulatory team (Pascal Werner, Robert Rogers) or a qualified regulatory affairs specialist.\n\n---\n\nSALES STRATEGY FOR MEDICAL DEVICES\n\nValue proposition development must answer: What clinical problem does this solve and for whom? Appropriate for complex, high-value products.\n- Distribution partnerships: established medical device distributors with existing hospital relationships. Faster market access, lower control over messaging and positioning.\n- GPO contracts: enables selling to a large pool of hospitals through a single framework contract. Required by many health systems for vendor approval, but GPO listing alone does not generate sales.\n- Digital and remote sales: appropriate for lower-complexity software products; reduces customer acquisition cost but may still require in-person support for VAC and IT reviews.\n- KOL strategy: engage clinical thought leaders for peer influence, publication opportunities, and speaking platforms. Physician-to-physician communication is more persuasive than vendor marketing.\n\nPhysician engagement: clinical champions within hospitals are essential but not sufficient — they must be connected to budget holders. CME (Continuing Medical Education) programs create structured engagement at scale. Medical conferences and professional society relationships build credibility. Do not confuse clinical advocacy with commercial decision-making authority.\n\nUS market dynamics: relationship-driven market; trust and clinical credibility are prerequisites. IDN (Integrated Delivery Network) consolidation means fewer but larger buying entities; IDN-level contracts create system-wide access but require complex multilateral negotiations. Medicare and Medicaid coverage decisions set reference points for commercial payer decisions.\n\nGerman market dynamics: Germany's GKV statutory insurance covers approximately 90% of the population; PKV private insurance covers the remainder. Hospital procurement follows public tender rules for above-threshold contracts. DiGA provides a structured but bureaucratic route for digital health applications. Local language, local relationships, and local regulatory knowledge are prerequisites — not optional.\n\n---\n\nCOMMON PAIN POINTS — WHY CLIENTS COME TO DHW\n\n1. Great product, sales team cannot sell it: Clinical excellence does not drive adoption. Unqualified reps, poor strategy, or hiring a sales team before understanding the buyer creates wasted effort. The commercial system must be designed before the sales team is scaled.\n\n2. Procurement and IT blocking the sale: Clinician enthusiasm is necessary but not sufficient. Products fail at procurement due to missing HIPAA or GDPR compliance documentation, MDR or 510(k) classification gaps, absence of EMR integration, or missing security certifications. Getting stuck in procurement limbo with interested buyers is the most common late-stage commercial failure.\n\n3. Pilot purgatory: Dozens of pilots, zero revenue. Pilots drain resources without a conversion path. Pre-define success metrics, budget source, and procurement plan before the pilot starts. A clinically successful pilot that does not convert to a paid contract is not a commercial success. Pear Therapeutics — the first FDA-approved digital therapeutic — collapsed in 2023 because insurers would not reimburse it due to lack of billing codes, despite clinical evidence and physician prescriptions.\n\n4. Builders and sellers do not speak the same language: Product teams build in silos; sales teams discover the gaps during demos. Working Backwards creates shared commercial context — product requirements flow from what the market actually needs to buy, not just what engineers want to build.\n\n5. Clinically excellent, commercially inviable: A product can be medically brilliant and commercially unsustainable if the reimbursement model is not established in parallel with the product. Billing codes, payer coverage, and VAC approval take time — start early.\n\n6. Local market hurdles: Germany's DiGA listing does not guarantee revenue. UK health AI firms with proven products have lost NHS procurement competitions to foreign vendors with stronger commercial packaging. Country-specific tender requirements, reimbursement criteria, and regulatory standards require dedicated local expertise.\n\n7. Scaling sales too early: Building a full commercial team before product-market fit burns cash. Validate the commercial thesis with founder-led or advisor-led sales first, then scale with a proven playbook.\n\n8. Misaligned incentives: A clinical champion without a financial champion is insufficient for purchasing decisions. Incentives must be aligned across clinical, financial, IT, and administrative stakeholders — all of whom are part of the procurement decision.\n\n---\n\nABOUT DHW AND WHEN TO SUGGEST A CALL\n\nDHW operates as a Manufacturer's Sales Rep Agency with three integrated service pillars:\n- Value Dynamics: reimbursement strategy, budget impact modeling, stakeholder analysis, HEOR (in partnership with Avalon Health Economics), VAC readiness\n- Market Development: GTM strategy, positioning, pricing, channel and distribution design, strategic partnerships for US and European market entry\n- Sales: direct pipeline building, hospital and health system engagement, procurement navigation, contract execution\n\nDHW AS A MANUFACTURER'S SALES REP AGENCY\nDHW positions itself as a Manufacturer's Sales Rep Agency for digital therapeutics (DTx), medical software, and Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) products in the US and EU/UK markets.
